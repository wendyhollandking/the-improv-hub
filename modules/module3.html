The basics

Where the funny lives

So far you've been building scenes and stories. Now we're going to talk about the people inside those scenes, because characters are where most of the comedy in improv actually comes from.

Think about the funniest moments you've seen on TV or in movies. Most of the time, what makes you laugh isn't a joke someone told. It's a person reacting in a way that's so specific and so committed that you can't help it. A character who is terrified of everything, or absurdly confident about something they're terrible at, or so enthusiastic that they can't contain themselves. The situation doesn't have to be funny. The character makes it funny.

How to build a character (fast)

In improv, you don't have time to write a backstory. You need to build a character in about two seconds. Here are three quick ways to do it.

1. Start with the body.
Change one physical thing about yourself and let everything else follow. Walk with a limp. Stand up super straight. Hunch your shoulders. Tilt your head. Wiggle when you talk. Pick something and commit to it completely. You'll be surprised how much a physical change affects the way you talk and think.

2. Pick one strong emotion or attitude.
Your character doesn't need to be complex. In fact, simpler is usually funnier. Pick one thing and crank it up:

  • Suspicious of everything
  • Ridiculously cheerful no matter what happens
  • Terrified of something very specific
  • Extremely competitive about something that doesn't matter
  • Obsessed with one topic and brings every conversation back to it

3. Give them a want.
What does your character want, right now, in this scene? Wanting something gives you a reason to talk, to react, and to push the scene forward. It can be anything: they want to leave, they want approval, they want a sandwich, they want to win.

You don't need all three. Even one is enough to make a character feel real. But combine a physical choice with an attitude and a want, and that character is going to be fun to watch.

Why exaggeration works

In real life, people are subtle. In improv, subtlety is invisible. If your character is a little bit nervous, the audience won't notice. If your character is so nervous that they're vibrating and speaking in a high-pitched squeak, now that's something.

The rule is: whatever you're doing, do more of it. If you're playing someone angry, be the angriest person who has ever lived. If you're playing someone who's in love, be so in love that birds land on your shoulders and background music starts playing.

This feels weird at first because it's the opposite of how you act in real life. But on stage (or in an improv circle), bigger reads better. The audience needs to be able to see and understand your character, and exaggeration is how you make that happen.

Staying in character

The hardest part of character work isn't starting the character. It's keeping it going, especially when something funny happens and you want to break.

  • Keep the physicality going. If your character walks funny, keep walking funny even when you're not the focus of the scene. Your body will help your brain stay in character.
  • React as the character, not as yourself. When something surprising happens, ask "how would this person react?" — not "what's the funniest thing I could say right now?"
  • Don't drop the voice. If you gave your character a specific way of talking, stick with it. Switching back to your normal voice breaks the illusion for everyone.
  • Let the character have opinions. Real people have opinions about everything. Your character should too. What does this person think about what's happening right now?

Quick check

Question 1

How does Ryan Stiles make his "giraffe getting hit with a tranquilizer dart" character work?

Right. Physicality is everything here. The whole joke lives in his body — the height, the slow collapse. Without full commitment, it's just a person shuffling around.

Not quite. The comedy in Ryan's character is entirely physical — how he uses his body to show height, movement, and the dart hit. Explaining or announcing the character would kill it.

Question 2

Why is the host (Greg) sometimes wrong about who the characters are — and the audience still laughs?

Exactly. The audience knows the characters from the start, so Greg's confusion is intentional comedy — watching him interpret the clues (and get it wrong) is the whole game.

Not quite. The audience knows the answers from the very beginning, so Greg's wrong guesses aren't a failure — they're the punchline. That gap between "we know" and "he doesn't" is where the comedy lives.

Game guide

Character Interview

Characters Solo-friendly ✨ AI playable

One person creates a character and sustains it under pressure. The other interviews them like it's a talk show. The interviewer asks questions, and the character has to stay fully committed to whoever they've decided to be.

This game is all about specificity and consistency. A vague character ("I'm a chef") runs out of interesting answers fast. A specific character ("I'm a chef who only cooks food from fictional planets and is deeply insulted when anyone calls it weird") has opinions, history, and surprises that can sustain a whole interview.

How to play

  1. One person is the interviewer, the other is the character.
  2. The character player decides on a character — based on a suggestion or made up on the spot. Commit to a voice, a physicality, and a point of view.
  3. The interviewer introduces the character and begins asking questions like it's a talk show.
  4. The character answers in character, making things up as they go. The key is consistency: build on what you've already established.
  5. The interviewer should listen and ask follow-up questions based on what the character says — not just run through a pre-planned list.
  6. After a few minutes, wrap up and swap roles.

Example

The interviewer says: "Please welcome our next guest, who I understand has just published a cookbook. Tell us about it."

The character (who had no idea they'd be a cookbook author) jumps in: "Yes, thank you for having me. The book is called Meals I've Eaten in Dreams and it's all recipes that came to me while I was sleeping. Chapter one is a lasagna that tastes like the color blue."

The interviewer follows up: "That's fascinating. How do your dream recipes translate to real ingredients?"

And the character keeps building — now they have to stay consistent with everything they've already established.

What to watch for

  • Consistency. If you said your character is afraid of spoons in the first answer, remember that for the rest of the interview.
  • Specifics. "I'm a chef" is boring. "I'm a chef who only cooks food from fictional planets" is interesting. The more specific your character, the more fun the interview is.
  • The interviewer matters too. A good interviewer listens and asks follow-up questions. "Tell me more about that" is often better than jumping to a new topic.
