The basics

Putting it all together

You know how to say "yes, and." You can build a character. Now comes the part where those skills combine into something bigger: an actual scene with a beginning, a middle, and an end.

A scene is just two or more characters in a situation, doing something together. That's it. But getting a scene to actually go somewhere — instead of just being two people talking in circles — takes a few specific skills.

Starting a scene

The beginning of a scene is where most people freeze up. You've been given a suggestion, someone's looking at you, and you have no idea what to say. Here's the good news: you don't need a great opening line. You just need to establish three things, and you can do them in any order.

Who are you to each other? Are you friends? Siblings? Strangers? Boss and employee? Two aliens? You can establish this with a single line: "Mom, I need to tell you something." Now the audience knows the relationship instantly.

Where are you? You don't have to say it out loud. You can show it — mime opening a car door, pretend to browse shelves, sit down like you're in a waiting room. Or just say it: "I love coming to this beach." Done.

What is happening? Something needs to be going on. Even something small. You're waiting for test results. You're planning a party. You're lost. You just found something weird. The "what" gives both players something to talk about and react to.

You don't have to establish all three in your first line. But the sooner the audience knows who, where, and what, the sooner they can relax and enjoy the scene.

Building the middle

Once the scene is started, the middle is where things develop. This is where a lot of beginners run into trouble — they either keep repeating the same idea without adding anything new, panic and introduce a random twist that has nothing to do with what's been established, or start asking their partner a bunch of questions instead of making statements.

The middle of a scene works best when you do two things:

Raise the stakes. Whatever is happening, make it matter more. If you're late for school, now you're late for the most important test of the year. If you found something weird in your locker, now it's making noises. You don't have to add a completely new situation. Just take what's already there and turn up the pressure.

Let your character be affected. The best scenes happen when characters actually care about what's going on and change because of it. If your scene partner gives you bad news, let it land. React. Get upset, or relieved, or confused. Characters who just shrug at everything aren't interesting to watch.

Ending a scene

Endings are tricky. In a class or practice setting, sometimes the host or teacher will call the scene. But if you need to find your own ending, look for one of these:

  • A character makes a decision. The whole scene has been building to a choice, and the character finally makes it.
  • A callback. Something from the beginning of the scene comes back in a new way. The audience loves this because it feels like the story came full circle.
  • A moment of realization. A character figures something out, and that understanding changes everything.
  • The biggest laugh. Sometimes the scene hits a peak and you can feel it. That's often a good time to end, before the energy drops.

Not every scene will have a clean ending, and that's okay. In improv, knowing when to stop is a skill that gets better with practice.

Listening and reacting

This keeps coming up because it really is that important. In scene work, listening doesn't just mean hearing the words your partner says. It means noticing their body language and physicality, the emotion behind what they're saying, what they're implying but not saying directly, and what has changed since the last line.

The best scene partners aren't the ones with the cleverest ideas. They're the ones who actually respond to what's happening in front of them instead of performing something they planned in their head.

Game guide

Freeze Tag

Scenes Warm-up

Two players start a scene. At any point, someone watching can yell "Freeze!" — both players freeze exactly where they are. The person who called freeze taps one player out, takes their exact physical position, and starts a completely different scene using that position as inspiration.

How to play

  1. Two players start a scene based on a suggestion.
  2. They play the scene, moving naturally and using their bodies.
  3. At any point, someone watching yells "Freeze!" and both players freeze in place.
  4. The caller taps one frozen player on the shoulder. That player sits down.
  5. The new player takes the exact physical position of the person they tapped out.
  6. The new player starts a completely different scene — using their position as the spark.
  7. The remaining player adapts to the new scene immediately.
  8. Play continues, with new people freezing in whenever they have an idea.

Example

Two players are in a scene where they're fishing — Player A leaning back holding an imaginary rod, Player B reaching forward with a net. Someone yells "Freeze!" and taps Player B. The new player takes B's reaching-forward position and says: "Okay, I'm going to pull this sword from the stone on the count of three."

Now it's a King Arthur scene — all because of the physical position.

What makes it work

  • Jump in fast. Don't wait for the perfect moment. If you have an idea, call freeze.
  • Use the body position. The whole point is that the pose inspires something new. Don't just ignore it and start talking about whatever you want.
  • Start the new scene immediately. Your first line should make the new scene clear. Don't take five seconds to figure it out.
  • Make physical choices in your scenes. Move around, gesture, interact with objects. The more physical you are, the more interesting positions you'll be in when someone calls freeze.

Starter prompts

  • Two people at a bus stop
  • Cooking a complicated meal together
  • Building a sandcastle
  • Two people in a canoe
  • Painting a mural
  • Training a very stubborn dog

Play: Story Director with AI

The AI is your Director. It'll set up a scene, drop in as a character, and then throw curveballs — fast-forward ten years, change the genre, add a surprise character, or flip the whole thing around. Your job is to adapt to whatever gets thrown at you without missing a beat.

How it works

  • The AI sets up a scene and describes what's happening
  • You respond with what your character does or says
  • The AI will sometimes play along as a scene partner, and sometimes interrupt as the Director with a twist
  • Adapt to every twist — don't fight the Director
  • Use what you learned in this module: establish who, where, and what quickly when a new scene starts
  • The scene will run about 8–10 exchanges then wrap up

Tips for handling the Director

  • When the Director changes something, go with it immediately. Don't pause to figure out the "right" response — just react
  • If the Director fast-forwards ten years, show how your character has changed
  • Let your character be affected by what happens — good or bad
  • When a new scene starts, establish who, where, and what as fast as you can

How'd that go?

No grades, no scores. Just a couple of things to think about:

  • → How did you handle the Director's twists? Did you adapt quickly, or did you need a beat to figure it out?
  • → Did the scene feel like it had a beginning, middle, and end — or did it just keep going in one direction?
  • → Was there a moment where the story surprised you or went somewhere you didn't expect?

The more you practice adapting to surprises, the better you get at thinking on your feet. That skill transfers to every other improv game you'll ever do. Try again with a different scene and see how your approach changes.
