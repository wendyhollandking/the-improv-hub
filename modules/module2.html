The basics

What is blocking, and why does it kill scenes?

"Blocking" is what happens when a player refuses or denies what their partner just offered. It sounds like this: "That's not a spaceship, it's a bus." Or: "I've never been to Paris." Or just: "What? No."

Blocking creates a dead end. The scene can't move forward because one player just erased the shared reality the other player was building. Now someone has to start over, and whatever momentum existed is gone.

It usually happens when someone gets scared of where the scene is going and tries to steer it back somewhere safer. The fix isn't to plan better — it's to trust that wherever things are going, you can work with it.

The "and" is the hard part

Most people get the "yes" part reasonably quickly. You accept what your partner says. Fine. What takes longer to learn is the "and" — because "and" requires you to actually contribute something of your own.

A lot of beginners say "yes" and then ask a question. "Yes! And... what should we do next?" That puts all the work back on your partner. The "and" means you take your turn. You add a new detail, a new character choice, a new development in the scene. Something that gives your partner something to work with.

Think of it like passing a ball. The "yes" is catching it. The "and" is throwing it back somewhere interesting.

Blocking (avoid this)

  • Denying what your partner said
  • Contradicting established facts
  • Changing the subject completely
  • Asking questions instead of making offers

Yes, And (aim for this)

  • Accepting what your partner established
  • Building on their specific details
  • Adding one new thing of your own
  • Giving your partner something to react to

Making your partner look good

One of the reasons Yes, And works is that it flips your instinct. Most people walk into a creative situation thinking about themselves: what should I do, how do I look, what's my move. Yes, And points your attention at your partner instead.

When you're focused on making your partner look good, a few things happen. You listen better, because you're actually paying attention to what they're offering. You say yes more naturally, because your job is to support their ideas, not compete with them. And the scene gets better for both of you, because now both players are supporting each other instead of trying to win.

The weird thing is: it works in the opposite direction too. When you stop trying to be the funniest person in the scene and start trying to make your partner funny, you usually end up funnier.

Game guide

Yes, And Scenes

Yes, And Scenes ✨ AI playable

Two players build a scene together, line by line, with a single constraint: every response has to accept what the other person just said and add something new. No denying. No redirecting. No questions that dump the work back on your partner. Just: yes, and.

There's no structure beyond that — the scene goes wherever the two players take it. You'll set a location, discover characters, find out what's happening. All of it emerges from the back-and-forth. The scenes that work best are the ones where both players trust each other enough to follow the offers.

How to play

  1. Pick or receive a prompt — a location, a relationship, a situation, whatever.
  2. One player opens with a line that establishes something about the scene.
  3. The other player accepts what was just said and adds one new thing.
  4. Keep going. Every line should acknowledge the previous one and move the scene forward.
  5. Let the scene find its own direction — don't plan ahead.
  6. After 6–8 exchanges, steer toward a natural ending.

Common pitfalls to watch for

  • Blocking. Directly or indirectly denying what your partner said. "I've never been here before" when they just said you come here all the time.
  • Asking questions instead of making offers. "What do you think we should do?" puts the work back on your partner. Make a statement instead.
  • Planning ahead. If you're already writing your next line while your partner is talking, you're not listening. The best offers come from what's actually happening in the scene, not from your mental screenplay.
  • Over-justifying. You don't need to explain everything. If your partner says there's a monkey in the room, just accept it and build on it. You don't need to explain why the monkey is there.

The point of the game

Yes, And Scenes aren't about being funny. They're about practicing the reflex of accepting and building, over and over, until it becomes automatic. The funny usually follows on its own — but only if you stop chasing it and focus on your partner.

  • → Did you find yourself wanting to block or redirect anything the AI said? What happened if you just went with it anyway?
