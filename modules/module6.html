The basics

From improv to script

You've been doing improv — making things up in the moment, saying yes, building scenes together with no plan. Sketch comedy takes the same skills and adds one thing: you write it down first.

That might sound like it removes all the fun, but it actually gives you a different kind of freedom. When you know what's coming, you can make every line land harder. You can time things perfectly. You can do a second take on a joke that only half worked. Improv teaches you how to find funny ideas; sketch writing teaches you how to build on them.

The best sketch writers are also good improvisers. And a lot of the best improv performers think like sketch writers — always noticing when something could be developed, heightened, and played all the way out.

What makes a sketch: the game

Every good sketch has a game — a single repeatable comic idea that the sketch plays with, over and over, in different ways. Not a plot, exactly. More like a rule that makes things funny every time it comes up.

In the "Substitute Teacher" sketch you watched in Module 5, the game is: a teacher mispronounces names and refuses to believe the students are saying them right. That's it. The whole sketch is variations on that one idea. In the "More Cowbell" sketch, the game is: one musician keeps adding cowbell to everything, and the producer thinks it makes it better. Same rule, different moments.

Finding the game is the most important skill in sketch writing. Ask yourself: what's the one weird thing happening in this scene? Can I do it again? Can I do it bigger? If yes, you probably have a game.

The structure of a sketch

Most sketches follow the same four-part shape, whether they're two minutes long or ten. Once you see it, you'll notice it everywhere.

  1. The setup. Establish the world, the characters, and what's normal — fast. Introduce the "game" as soon as possible so the audience knows what they're watching. A good setup is quick and clear; a sketch that spends too long explaining itself runs out of room to be funny.
  2. The game. Play the game. The weird thing happens again, in a different way. And again. Each time should feel slightly more extreme than the last. This is the middle of the sketch, and it's where most of the laughs live.
  3. The heighten. The game escalates. Things get more absurd, the stakes get higher, the characters get more committed to their position. The audience has been on a slow escalator, and now it's going faster.
  4. The button. The very last moment — usually one final joke that lands as a punctuation mark. The button doesn't resolve everything; it just ends things at the highest possible point. Leave on a laugh, not an explanation.

Finding your sketch idea

The best sketch ideas start with a "what if" question. Not "what if something crazy happened" — something more specific. What if the one rule of this situation were slightly different? What if this normal thing were taken completely literally? What if this character believed something that nobody else believes?

There are three reliable places to look:

  • The what-if. Start with a rule, flip it, and follow it logically. What if customer service was actually helpful — terrifyingly, relentlessly helpful? What if a museum tour guide were completely wrong about everything? The logic of the idea generates the jokes.
  • The character. Some sketches are just about a very specific person who sees the world in a very specific way. Every situation they're in becomes funny because of how they process it. The character is the game.
  • The normal + the weird. Take something completely ordinary and put one strange thing in it. A job interview is normal; a job interview where the interviewer keeps falling asleep is a sketch. The ordinary makes the weird funnier; the weird makes the ordinary visible.

Writing vs. performing

Sketch writing and sketch performing are two different skills, and you can be great at one without being great at the other — though both get better with practice.

When you're writing, your job is to find the game and build the structure. When you're performing, your job is to commit completely to the reality of the scene. Over-performing a sketch is just as deadly as under-writing it. The best sketch performers aren't playing "isn't this funny?" — they're playing "this is completely normal and I will defend it with my life." The funnier the premise, the more straight you should play it.

In this module, you'll write. There's a place for performing, but building the structure is the skill — and it's one that transfers everywhere.

How to build a sketch

This isn't a game you play in a circle — it's a process you follow when you're writing. Each step builds on the last. Work through them in order, and by the time you reach step 5, you'll have the outline of a real sketch.

Step 1: Start with a what-if

Write down a question that starts with "what if." It should describe a situation where one specific thing is different from normal. Not "what if everything was weird" — that's too vague. "What if a GPS gave terrible life advice?" That's a game.

Give yourself three minutes. Write as many what-ifs as you can without judging any of them. Pick the one that makes you want to keep going.

Step 2: Identify the game

Look at your what-if and find the single repeatable rule inside it. The game should be simple enough to say in one sentence. "Every time X happens, Y happens" is the pattern you're looking for.

Ask yourself: can I do this at least three times in a row? Does each repetition leave room to escalate? If the answer to both is yes, you have a game. If the idea only works once, it's a joke — not a sketch.

Step 3: Write the setup

Establish who's in the scene, where they are, and what's normal — as fast as possible. The audience needs just enough to understand what the game is when it first appears. A good setup usually takes about a minute of screen time, sometimes less.

Introduce the game clearly the first time. Don't hint at it or tease it — show it. The first time the game happens, the audience should think "wait, is that a thing?" The second time, they should think "oh, it's definitely a thing." The third time, they're already laughing before it lands because they know it's coming.

Step 4: Heighten three times

Play the game three more times. Each time, make it bigger: higher stakes, more extreme behavior, a more ridiculous situation. By the third heighten, things should feel a little out of control in the best possible way.

The rule of three from Module 5 applies here: setup once, play it twice, then deliver the third version as the escalation peak. The third heighten is where the biggest laugh usually lives.

Step 5: Find the button

The button is the very last moment of the sketch — a final joke, image, or line that ends things at the highest possible point. It doesn't explain or resolve anything. It just punctuates.

Good buttons often call back to something from earlier in the sketch. They take the final escalation to its logical (or illogical) extreme. Or they introduce one last unexpected twist that recontextualizes everything. What they never do: explain the joke, sum up what happened, or end on a soft note. Leave on a laugh, cut to black.
