The basics

Why some things are funny

By now you've played improv games, built characters, and created scenes. You've probably noticed that some moments get big laughs and others don't, and it's not always obvious why. The truth is, comedy follows patterns. Once you learn to recognize them, you can use them on purpose instead of just hoping something lands.

You don't need to memorize a comedy textbook. There are really just a few patterns that show up over and over in improv, sketch comedy, stand-up, and even funny conversations with your friends.

Pattern 1: The rule of three

This is probably the most common pattern in comedy. Set up a pattern with two normal things, then break it with a third thing that's unexpected.

  • "I packed everything I need for the trip: socks, toothbrush, and my emotional baggage."
  • "She's great at three things: math, soccer, and convincing her parents she's studying when she's definitely not."
  • "My dog knows three commands: sit, stay, and how to guilt-trip me into giving him my dinner."

The first two items set up an expectation. The third one breaks it. Your brain was following a pattern and then the pattern changes — and that surprise is what makes you laugh.

In improv, the rule of three shows up constantly. If something funny happens in a scene, do it again (the audience enjoys the callback), then do it a third time with a twist (the audience loses it because they saw it coming but not quite like that).

Pattern 2: Escalation

Escalation means taking something and making it bigger, more extreme, or more absurd with each repetition. You already practiced this in Fortunately/Unfortunately. The idea is simple: whatever just happened, the next thing is more.

  • A character is nervous. Then very nervous. Then so nervous they're vibrating.
  • A small problem becomes a medium problem becomes a catastrophe.
  • Someone tells a small lie that gets bigger and bigger until it's completely out of control.

Escalation works because the audience gets on the ride with you. Each step is a little more ridiculous than the last, and there's a joy in watching things spiral. The key is to escalate gradually. If you jump straight to the most extreme version, there's nowhere left to go.

Pattern 3: The callback

A callback is when you reference something from earlier in the scene (or the show) that the audience has already laughed at. It's an inside joke between you and the audience, and they love it because it makes them feel like they're in on something.

If early in a scene someone mentions that the coffee tastes weird, and then twenty minutes later in a completely different scene someone takes a sip of something and makes a face, the audience will connect those moments and laugh because they remember.

Callbacks work best when:

  • There's been enough time between the original moment and the callback that it feels like a surprise
  • The callback isn't exactly the same as the original — it's the same idea in a new context
  • You don't overdo it. One or two per show is plenty

Pattern 4: Specificity

This isn't a "pattern" exactly, but it's one of the biggest comedy tips you'll ever get: specific is always funnier than vague.

Vague (not funny)

  • He drove a car
  • She ate some food
  • They went to a store
  • He played a song

Specific (funny)

  • He drove a 1997 minivan with a bumper sticker that said "My other car is also terrible"
  • She ate an entire sleeve of Oreos standing in front of the open fridge at 2 AM
  • They went to Costco
  • He played the Macarena

The specificity gives the audience a picture in their head, and a picture is almost always funnier than a blur. When you need to name something in a scene, pick a specific thing — not "an animal" but "a very judgmental pelican."

Timing and reactions

The last piece of the comedy puzzle isn't about what you say. It's about when you say it, and what you do when you're not talking.

  • The pause. Sometimes the funniest thing you can do is nothing. If something ridiculous just happened, a beat of silence before you respond lets the moment land. The audience needs a second to process, and the pause gives them permission to laugh.
  • The reaction. In a lot of improv scenes, the biggest laughs don't come from the person doing the funny thing — they come from the other person's reaction to it. A slow blink, a jaw drop, a confused head tilt. Never underestimate the power of just reacting honestly.
  • Knowing when not to talk. Beginners often feel like they need to keep talking to keep the scene alive. But some of the best moments in improv are silent. A look between two characters can say more than a paragraph of dialogue.

Game guide

Half-Life

Scenes Comedy 👥 2+ players

Two players perform a short scene, then replay it in half the time. Then half again. And again. Each replay hits the same key beats but gets faster, more frantic, and more exaggerated — until by the final round it's basically two people speed-screaming the highlights while flailing around. One of the most reliably hilarious formats in improv.

How to play

  1. Two players perform a short scene (about 2 minutes) with a few clear, memorable moments.
  2. When the scene ends, the host says "Half-life!" and the players replay it in about 1 minute, hitting the same beats but faster.
  3. "Half-life!" again — 30 seconds.
  4. "Half-life!" again — 15 seconds.
  5. Final round: 7 seconds. Usually just the biggest moments crammed into chaos.
  6. Optional: 3 seconds, or a single frozen pose.

Example

Original scene: Two friends go fishing, one catches a boot, they argue about whether to keep it, a fish jumps out and steals the boot.

By the final round: cast — "BOOT!" — "MINE!" — "NO!" — fish steals boot — end.

Why this game is great for learning comedy

Half-Life forces you to figure out which moments in your scene are the most important and the most funny. Each replay, you have to cut the stuff that doesn't matter and keep what gets laughs. By the end, you've distilled the scene down to its purest, funniest moments — and the speed itself becomes part of the comedy.

Starter prompts

  • Two people assembling furniture with terrible instructions
  • A surprise birthday party that goes horribly wrong
  • Two siblings arguing over the TV remote
  • A job interview where the interviewer keeps falling asleep
  • A student presenting a book report on a book they clearly didn't read
  • A bank robbery where the robber is extremely polite
