The basics

What is improv?

Improvisation means making it up as you go — no script, no plan, no safety net. In improv, you and your partner (or group) create scenes, characters, and stories in real time, building on whatever each other says and does.

That sounds terrifying. It's also kind of addictive once you get over the initial panic. The whole point is that there's no wrong answer, because there was never a right one to begin with.

The rules of improv

Improv has a few core principles that show up across every game, scene, and style. You'll come back to these again and again throughout the modules.

  1. Yes, And. The most important rule. Accept what your partner offers — their ideas, their characters, their reality — and add something new. This is the engine that keeps scenes moving. (We'll go deep on this in Module 2.)
  2. Make your partner look good. Your job isn't to be the funniest person on stage. It's to make your partner shine. When everyone's focused on making their partner look good, everyone ends up looking great.
  3. Commit. Whatever character you're playing, wherever the scene goes — do it fully. A half-hearted choice is just awkward. A fully committed choice, even a strange one, is interesting and usually funny.
  4. Listen. Not "wait for your turn to talk." Actually listen. The best stuff in improv happens when you're genuinely reacting to what just happened, not executing the line you were already planning.
  5. There are no mistakes. A fumbled word, an unexpected direction, an idea that goes sideways — that's not failure, it's material. The most experienced improvisers aren't better because they make fewer mistakes. They're better because they stopped caring when they do.

Why being silly matters

Getting comfortable being silly is the foundation of everything else in improv. Not because improv is only about being ridiculous (it isn't), but because learning to play without fear of judgment is what makes everything else possible.

You can't take risks if you're worried about looking stupid. You can't commit to a weird character if you're holding part of yourself back. You can't say yes to your partner's left-field idea if your first instinct is to protect yourself from embarrassment.

The first skill to develop in improv isn't a technique. It's a mindset: this is a space where being dumb, strange, wrong, and weird is not only fine — it's the point.

Quick check

Question 1

When one player "blocks" in an improv scene — refusing or contradicting what their partner just said — what happens?

Exactly. Blocking cuts off the shared reality the two players were building — the scene can't move forward when one person keeps saying no.

Not quite. Blocking doesn't create useful conflict or better ideas — it just shuts the scene down. The whole thing depends on both players accepting each other's offers.

Question 2

Why do improvisers practice listening so carefully — not just waiting for their turn to talk?

Right. If you're planning your next line instead of listening, you'll miss what your partner just gave you — which is almost always better than what you were planning anyway.

Not quite. Improv isn't about executing a plan or catching mistakes — it's about genuinely reacting in real time. Listening is what makes "yes, and" actually possible.

Game guide

Fortunately / Unfortunately

Yes, And Solo-friendly ✨ AI playable

Two players build a story together by alternating lines. One player always says "Fortunately..." and the other always says "Unfortunately..." — flipping the story's fortune back and forth with every exchange.

The game starts with a setup sentence (usually read aloud by someone outside the game, or by the first player before their first line). From there, each line either saves the situation or makes things worse — which creates a natural rhythm of escalation and relief that's surprisingly easy to get hooked on.

How to play

  1. Read or announce the setup prompt — this establishes the story's starting situation.
  2. Decide who goes first. Player A always says "Unfortunately..." and Player B always says "Fortunately..."
  3. Player A begins: "Unfortunately, [something bad happens]."
  4. Player B continues: "Fortunately, [something saves the day or makes it better]."
  5. Keep alternating. Each line should connect directly to the previous one.
  6. After 8–10 exchanges, steer the story toward a conclusion.

Example

Setup A student brings a dinosaur egg to school for show-and-tell, and it starts to hatch in the middle of class.
Unfortunately The baby dinosaur hatched directly on top of the teacher's laptop.
Fortunately The dinosaur immediately bonded with the school librarian, who has always wanted a pet.
Unfortunately It turns out it's not a baby — it's full-grown and still growing.
Fortunately The gymnasium is conveniently the exact size and shape of a dinosaur habitat.
Unfortunately Gym class was already scheduled for that period.

Why this game is great for beginners

The alternating structure does a lot of the work for you. You always know what word to start with, which means you can focus entirely on connecting to what your partner just said and finding an unexpected angle. It's great practice for "yes, and" thinking without requiring anyone to be "in character" — the story itself is the scene.

How'd that go?

No grades, no scores. Just a couple of things to think about:

  • → Did your lines connect to what was just said, or did you find yourself wanting to go off in a different direction?
  • → Was there a moment where the story surprised you — where it went somewhere you didn't expect?
  • → Did escalating feel natural, or did it feel like you had to force it?

If you want, try it again with a different prompt or swap who goes first. The same setup can go in very different directions depending on how you approach the first exchange — it's worth finding out.
